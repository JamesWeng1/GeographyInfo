1. Population & Settlement

Understanding human populations, migration, and settlement patterns

Key Vocabulary

Birth Rate

The number of live births per thousand people per year.

Death Rate

The number of deaths per thousand people per year.

Natural Increase

When birth rate exceeds death rate (population grows without migration).

Population Density

Number of people per square kilometre (people/km²).

Dependency Ratio

Ratio of dependents (young + old) to the working-age population.

Migration

Movement of people from one place to another to live.

Urbanisation

Increase in proportion of population living in urban areas.

Rural-Urban Migration

Movement from countryside to cities.

Megacity

City with population over 10 million people.

Settlement Hierarchy

Ranking of settlements by size/services (hamlet, village, town, city).

Common Past Paper Questions

Question 1

Explain two reasons for high rates of rural-urban migration in developing countries. (4 marks)

Tip: Consider both push (from rural) and pull (to urban) factors.

Question 2

Describe the likely population pyramid for a country with a high birth rate and decreasing death rate. (3 marks)

Tip: Draw a pyramid with wide base (young) and narrowing upwards.

Question 3

Compare the challenges of managing population growth in densely populated and sparsely populated areas. (6 marks)

Tip: Address issues like resource pressure vs. service provision.

Question 4

Analyze how the functions of a settlement might change as it grows in size. (5 marks)

Tip: Consider changes from basic services to higher-order services.

Case Studies

Japan - Aging Population

Overview

Japan has one of the world's oldest populations, with over 28% aged 65+. This creates significant demographic challenges.

Causes

  • Very low birth rate (1.34 births per woman)
  • High life expectancy (84 years)
  • Traditional societal values changing
  • High cost of living and child-rearing

Impacts

  • Shrinking workforce and economic growth
  • Increased pressure on pension systems
  • Healthcare services under strain
  • Rural depopulation and abandoned towns

Responses

  • Robotics and automation to compensate for labor shortages
  • Encouraging immigration (slow policy change)
  • Financial incentives for families to have children
  • Raising retirement age from 65 to 70

Singapore - Population Management

Overview

A small city-state with high population density that has implemented proactive population policies.

Historical Context

In the 1960s-80s, Singapore had a "Stop at Two" policy to control rapid population growth. Today it faces declining birth rates.

Current Challenges

  • Very low fertility rate (1.14 in 2020)
  • Reliance on foreign labor
  • Limited physical space
  • Aging population similar to Japan

Policies & Strategies

  • Pro-natalist policies: Baby bonuses, tax relief, extended maternity leave
  • Immigration: Controlled skilled migration to maintain workforce
  • Urban planning: High-rise living, efficient public transport
  • Social engineering: Promotion of marriage and family values

Results

While birth rates remain low, Singapore has maintained economic growth through selective immigration and efficient urban management.

2. The Natural Environment

Physical geography including rivers, coasts, tectonics, and climate

Key Vocabulary

Erosion

The wearing away and removal of material by water, wind, or ice.

Transportation

The movement of eroded material.

Deposition

Dropping of material when a river or wave loses energy.

Meander

A pronounced bend in a river.

Ox-bow Lake

Crescent-shaped lake formed from a cut-off meander.

Longshore Drift

Zigzag movement of material along a coast by waves.

Spit

Long, narrow finger of sand/shingle projecting from coastline.

Plate Tectonics

Theory that Earth's crust is divided into moving plates.

Epicentre

Point on Earth's surface directly above earthquake focus.

Richter Scale

Logarithmic scale measuring earthquake magnitude.

Common Past Paper Questions

Question 1

Explain how a river's characteristics change from its source to its mouth. (6 marks)

Tip: Discuss gradient, velocity, discharge, sediment size, and valley shape.

Question 2

Describe the process of longshore drift and its role in forming coastal landforms. (4 marks)

Tip: Include direction of swash/backwash and formation of spits/bars.

Question 3

Compare the characteristics of shield and composite volcanoes. (5 marks)

Tip: Consider shape, lava type, eruption style, and plate boundary location.

Question 4

Evaluate the effectiveness of different coastal management strategies. (7 marks)

Tip: Compare hard vs soft engineering, considering cost, effectiveness, and environmental impact.

Case Studies

The Ganges River - Flood Management

Overview

The Ganges is a major river flowing through India and Bangladesh, prone to seasonal flooding that affects millions.

Physical Characteristics

  • Length: 2,525 km
  • Source: Gangotri Glacier in Himalayas
  • Mouth: Bay of Bengal (forms world's largest delta)
  • Basin population: Over 400 million people

Causes of Flooding

  • Monsoon rainfall: Heavy July-September rains
  • Glacial melt: Increased meltwater from Himalayas
  • Deforestation: Reduced interception in catchment
  • Delta location: Flat land with slow drainage
  • Urbanisation: Impermeable surfaces increase runoff

Impacts

  • Positive: Deposits fertile silt for agriculture
  • Negative: Loss of life, property damage, displacement
  • Economic: Damage to infrastructure, loss of crops
  • Social: Spread of waterborne diseases, homelessness

Management Strategies

  • Embankments (levees): Over 3,500 km along the river
  • Flood warnings: Improved forecasting systems
  • Afforestation: Planting in upper catchment to reduce runoff
  • Dam construction: Farakka Barrage for flow regulation
  • Community preparedness: Emergency shelters, evacuation plans

Boscastle Flood (2004)

Overview

A flash flood in the Cornish village of Boscastle on 16 August 2004 caused by extreme rainfall.

Causes

  • Extreme rainfall: 200mm in 4 hours (3 months' average)
  • Steep valleys: Rapid surface runoff
  • Saturated ground: Previous wet weather
  • Confluence: Three rivers meet at Boscastle
  • Narrow river channel: Easily blocked by debris

Impacts

  • No deaths but 58 properties flooded
  • 75 cars, 5 buildings swept into sea
  • Extensive damage to infrastructure
  • Tourism disruption (main local industry)
  • Insurance costs: £20 million+

Responses & Management

  • Immediate: Helicopter rescue of 150 people
  • Engineering: River channel widened and deepened
  • Defenses: New flood walls and culverts
  • Early warning: Improved flood warning system
  • Land use: Car park raised above flood level
  • Monitoring: Regular dredging to prevent blockages

Why Study Boscastle?

Excellent example of how physical and human factors combine to create a flood hazard, and how both hard and soft engineering can reduce future risk.

3. Economic Development

Industry, globalization, development, and sustainability

Key Vocabulary

Primary Industry

Extracting raw materials (farming, mining, fishing).

Secondary Industry

Manufacturing raw materials into products.

Tertiary Industry

Providing services (teaching, retail, transport).

Quaternary Industry

High-tech, research, information services.

Employment Structure

Proportion of people working in each sector.

Informal Sector

Economy not taxed/monitored by government.

Multinational Corporation (MNC)

Company operating in many countries.

Globalisation

Growing interconnection of countries worldwide.

HDI

Human Development Index - composite measure using life expectancy, education, income.

Sustainable Development

Development meeting present needs without compromising future generations.

Common Past Paper Questions

Question 1

Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of multinational corporations (MNCs) for host countries. (6 marks)

Tip: Consider employment, technology transfer vs profit repatriation, environmental standards.

Question 2

Explain how the employment structure of a country changes as it develops. (5 marks)

Tip: Describe transition from primary-dominated to service-based economy.

Question 3

Compare the strengths and limitations of GDP per capita and HDI as measures of development. (4 marks)

Tip: GDP measures wealth only; HDI includes social factors.

Question 4

Evaluate strategies to promote sustainable development in rapidly industrializing countries. (7 marks)

Tip: Consider renewable energy, conservation, education, and appropriate technology.

4. Geographical Skills

Mapwork, data analysis, and fieldwork techniques

Key Vocabulary

Relief

The height and shape of the land.

Contour Line

Line joining points of equal height on a map.

Grid Reference

Location defined by numbers (4-figure for area, 6-figure for point).

Bearing

Direction measured in degrees from north (000°).

Scale

Relationship between map distance and real distance.

Choropleth Map

Map using color shades to show data variations.

Dispersed Settlement

Buildings spread out (often rural).

Nucleated Settlement

Buildings clustered around central point.

Linear Settlement

Buildings arranged in a line along road/river.

Land Use Map

Map showing how land in an area is used.

Common Past Paper Questions

Question 1

Calculate the gradient between point A (125m) and point B (75m) that are 2km apart on a map. (3 marks)

Tip: Gradient = vertical difference / horizontal distance.

Question 2

Describe the advantages of using a choropleth map to display population density data. (3 marks)

Tip: Consider visual clarity, pattern recognition, and comparison ease.

Question 3

Explain how you would conduct a fieldwork investigation to measure changes along a river's course. (5 marks)

Tip: Include site selection, equipment (callipers, meter ruler, float), methodology, and safety.

Question 4

Analyze the likely settlement pattern in an area with contour lines close together and sparse roads. (4 marks)

Tip: Consider physical constraints on settlement development.
